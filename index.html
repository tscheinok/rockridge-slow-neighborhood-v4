The Problem

Navigation apps turned our neighborhood from residential to thoroughfare

The Rockridge "trapezoid" ‚Äî the residential area bounded by College, Claremont, Telegraph, and Alcatraz Avenues ‚Äî has become a major cut-through corridor. Waze, Google Maps, and gig-economy routing send thousands of commuter vehicles daily onto streets never designed for that volume.

Children at Risk

There's a preschool on Colby Street. During rush hour, over 400 cars per hour race past ‚Äî residents can't safely exit their driveways, and families can't safely cross the street.

Bike Boulevard Failure

NACTO guidance establish as a ceiling 50 cars per hour and 2,000 per day on a bike boulevard. During rush hour we hit 400 ‚Äî 8√ó the hourly limit ‚Äî and daily volumes reach 4,000, 2√ó the daily threshold.

Setting the Record Straight

Claims vs. evidence

Misinformation has circulated in the neighborhood. Here's what the data and OakDOT's own assessments actually show.

Claim

"Diverters don't reduce traffic"

Evidence

UC Berkeley ITS modeled a 60% reduction in cut-through traffic. Diverters are the global standard for this exact problem.

Claim

"Better solutions have been identified"

Evidence

OakDOT evaluated alternatives. OakDOT staff noted that turn restrictions alone "would be unlikely to have a meaningful impact on total volume." No better option within budget was found.

Claim

"This will just push traffic to other streets"

Evidence

The ITS model shows 62nd St increases from 8 to 17 cars/hr ‚Äî a manageable change. The net effect is a 60% neighborhood-wide reduction. That's why it's a pilot: to test, measure, and adjust.

Claim

"The community rejected diverters"

Evidence

The Working Group was disbanded by OakDOT's Director after opposition members acted in "bad faith." The Director explicitly stated: "the diverters were never taken off the table."

(B) Barcelona Superblocks

Barcelona's superblocks achieved a 25% reduction in through-traffic and a 17% decrease in NO‚ÇÇ pollution by restricting vehicle access to local-only within multi-block zones. Streets formerly dominated by cars became public spaces for walking, cycling, and community life ‚Äî with no major infrastructure cost. The model is now replicated in cities worldwide.

(C) Traffic Treatments Effectiveness

Diverters deliver 65% traffic volume reduction ‚Äî the highest of any treatment ‚Äî and score 260% on combined effectiveness (impact √ó affordability), far above the 100% threshold. By comparison, signage alone achieves just 3% reduction. Data compiled from FHWA, NACTO, and peer-reviewed transportation research.

The "Pride Pilot" (81st Ave)

A 3-week diverter pilot in East Oakland that reduced cut-through traffic by 65‚Äì75% using a single diagonal traffic filter. Speeds dropped by up to 4 mph near East Oakland Pride Elementary, and the school principal reported dramatically calmer conditions ‚Äî proof that diverters work quickly and decisively.

The "Ney Avenue" Diverter

Oakland's own study showing diverters successfully redirected non-local traffic off residential streets while preserving full access for residents. The physical barriers eliminated shortcutting without displacing meaningful volume to adjacent streets.

The Berkeley Model (Hillegass Ave)

Just blocks north of our neighborhood, Berkeley used diverters on Hillegass Avenue to keep traffic volumes below 1,500 ADT on their bicycle boulevard. The same street crosses into Oakland and immediately jumps to nearly 2,000 ADT ‚Äî demonstrating exactly what happens without traffic calming.

NACTO Case Study: Channing Way Diverter

NACTO documents how Berkeley's Channing Way diverter eliminated cut-through traffic while enabling safe bicycle crossings at a major intersection. The project uses loop detectors and protected platforms ‚Äî a model for combining traffic reduction with active transportation safety.

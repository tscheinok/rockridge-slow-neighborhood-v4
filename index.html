The Problem

Navigation apps turned our neighborhood into a highway

The Rockridge "trapezoid" ‚Äî the residential area bounded by College, Claremont, Telegraph, and Alcatraz Avenues ‚Äî has become a major cut-through corridor. Waze, Google Maps, and gig-economy routing send thousands of commuter vehicles daily onto streets never designed for that volume.

Children at Risk

There's a preschool on Colby Street. During rush hour, over 400 cars per hour race past ‚Äî residents can't safely exit their driveways, and families can't safely cross the street.

Bike Boulevard Failure

Colby is Oakland's designated bicycle boulevard ‚Äî a primary bike corridor connecting neighborhoods across North Oakland and South Berkeley. NACTO guidance says no more than 50 cars per hour and 2,000 per day on a bike boulevard. During rush hour we hit 400 ‚Äî 8√ó the hourly limit ‚Äî and daily volumes reach 4,000, 2√ó the daily threshold.

Setting the Record Straight

Claims vs. evidence

Misinformation has circulated in the neighborhood. Here's what the data and OakDOT's own assessments actually show.

Claim

"Diverters don't reduce traffic"

Evidence

UC Berkeley ITS modeled a 60% reduction in cut-through traffic. Diverters are the global standard for this exact problem.

Claim

"Better solutions have been identified"

Evidence

OakDOT evaluated alternatives. OakDOT staff noted that turn restrictions alone "would be unlikely to have a meaningful impact on total volume." No better option within budget was found.

Claim

"This will just push traffic to other streets"

Evidence

The ITS model shows 62nd St increases from 8 to 17 cars/hr ‚Äî a manageable change. The net effect is a 60% neighborhood-wide reduction. That's why it's a pilot: to test, measure, and adjust.

Claim

"The community rejected diverters"

Evidence

The Working Group was disbanded by OakDOT's Director after opposition members acted in "bad faith." The Director explicitly stated: "the diverters were never taken off the table."

The "Pride Pilot" (81st Ave)

A 3-week demonstration project in East Oakland that successfully reduced traffic volumes by 65-75% using a diagonal traffic filter. The project, located near East Oakland Pride Elementary, reduced cut-through traffic while maintaining local access. Traffic speeds decreased by up to 4 mph, and the school principal reported significantly calmer traffic conditions.

The "Ney Avenue" Diverter

A neighborhood traffic calming study examining the effectiveness of traffic diverters in reducing cut-through traffic on residential streets. This project demonstrates how physical barriers can successfully redirect non-local traffic while preserving neighborhood access.

The Berkeley Model (Hillegass Ave)

A comprehensive bicycle boulevard design guide from 2000 that outlines proven tools and strategies for creating safe, efficient bicycle routes through residential neighborhoods. The guide includes traffic calming techniques, intersection treatments, and design principles that reduce vehicle volumes and speeds while improving safety for all users.
